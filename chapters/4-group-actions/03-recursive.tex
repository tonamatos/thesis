\section{Recursive constructions}\label{sec:recursive_constructions}

\subsection{Balanceable graphs}\label{subsec:amoebas_bal}

Ramsey's classical theorem for finite graphs states that, for a fixed graph $G$, there is a large enough complete graph $K_n$ such that no matter how the edges of $K_n$ are coloured (in say two colours), one can find a monochromatic copy of $G$ inside of $K_n$.
Suppose one wishes to find a copy of $G$ inside the coloured graph $K_n$, but instead of monochromatic, with half of the edges (possibly rounding if $G$ has an odd number of edges) in one colour, and the other half in the remaining colour.
Clearly, one needs to impose additional conditions on the colourings of $K_n$, namely, that each colour class is ``well represented'' (since otherwise one could colour $K_n$ in a single colour, making it impossible to find a non-trivial graph with the desired property) and one needs additional conditions on the structure of $G$.
I do not go into the details of what those properties are, beyond what is needed to state and expand the results from my own research, as well as tie these concepts to the other ideas in this section.
The interested reader will find a thorough discussion and complete characterization of these graphs in \cite[Section~2]{unavoidable_chromatic}.

\begin{definition}\label{def:balancing_num}
    Suppose that a finite graph $G$ has an edge-colouring $c\colon E(G)\to2$ in two colours.
    This coloured graph is called \emph{balanced} if the number of edges of each colour differ by at most one; that is, if $G$ has an even number of edges, there are exactly as many edges of each colour.

    A finite graph $G$ is called \emph{balanceable} if in any $2$-colouring of the complete graph $K_n$, with sufficiently many edges of each colour and $n$ sufficiently large, one can find a balanced copy of $G$.

    The \emph{balancing number} $\bal(n,G)$ of a balanceable graph $G$ is the maximum number $k$ of edges for which there is a $2$-coloring of $K_n$ with exactly $k$ edges in one colour without balanced copies of $G$.
\end{definition}

In other words, any $2$-coloring of $K_n$ with more than $\bal(n,G)$ edges in each color contains a balanced copy of $G$.

\begin{theorem}[{\cite[Corollary~2.5]{unavoidable_chromatic}}]\label{thm:bal_char}
    A finite graph $G$ is balanceable if and only if:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item there are disjoint $X,Y\subseteq V(G)$ such that $V(G)=X\cup Y$ and there are either $\left\lfloor\frac12k\right\rfloor$ or $\left\lceil\frac12k\right\rceil$ edges between the vertices of $X$ and $Y$; and
        
        \item there is $W\subseteq V(G)$ such that there are either $\left\lfloor\frac12k\right\rfloor$ or $\left\lceil\frac12k\right\rceil$ edges between vertices of $W$.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

We can study more specific aspects of graphs than whether they are balanceable or not.
Interestingly, the balancing number may or may not depend on $n$, as the following examples demonstrate.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:balC_4_is_one}
    The $4$-cycle, $C_4$, has balancing number $1$.
    More specifically, $\bal(n,C_4)=1$ whenever $n\geq4$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}    
    Indeed, it is obvious that the balancing number cannot be less than 1, as $C_4$ has four edges, and hence a balanced copy needs two edges of each colour.

    As for the upper bound, let $n\geq 4$ and fix an arbitrary edge colouring $c\colon E(K_n)\to\{\text{red},\text{blue}\}$ with at least two edges of each colour.
    First, suppose there is a vertex $x\in V(K_n)$ all of whose neighbours are joined with edges of the same colour (say red).
    Then, if there are two blue edges in $K_n$ that intersect, they form a blue path of vertices $p_0,p_1,p_2$, neither of which can be $x$ by assumption.
    The edges $(x,p_0),(p_0,p_1),(p_1,p_2),(p_2,x)$ are red, blue, blue, red, respectively, and thus form a balanced copy of $C_4$.
    If no two blue edges intersect, then the edges joining the endpoints of two particular blue edges (of which there must be at least two) must be all red, which we use to form a balanced copy of $C_4$.
    
    Now, consider the case when every vertex has red and blue incident edges.
    Continue by fixing a red edge $xy$ and partitioning the vertices $w\in V(K_n)\setminus\{x,y\}$ into four classses depending on the value of the pair $(c(wx),c(wy))$.
    There are two colours, hence four possible values for this pair; let us abbreviate the classes as $RR$ for those vertices $w$ with pair $(\text{red},\text{red})$ and similarly, $RB,BR$ and $BB$.
    By assumption, $x$ and $y$ have blue incident edges and thus $V(K_n)\setminus\{x,y\}\notin\{RR,RB,BR\}$.
    If $V(K_n)\setminus\{x,y\}=BB$, then any red edge different from $xy$ must be disjoint from $xy$ and forms a balanced copy of $C_4$ by connecting said edge to $xy$ (via blue edges).
    We can therefore assume that at least two of the four classes are nonempty, giving six sub-cases to analyze:

    If there are $w\in RR$ and $z\in BB$, or if there are $w\in RB$ and $z\in BR$, then $(x,w,y,z)$ is a balanced $4$-cycle; if $w\in RB$ and $z\in BB$, then either $(x,w,z,y)$ (whenever $wz$ is blue) or $(x,y,w,z)$ (whenever $wz$ is red) is a balanced $4$-cycle; the case $w\in BR$ and $z\in BB$ is symmetric by flipping the possible colours of $wz$; if $w\in RR$, $z\in RB\cup BR$ and $wz$ is blue, then either $(x,y,z,w)$ (whenever $z\in RB$) or $(x,y,w,z)$ (whenever $z\in BR$) is a balanced $4$-cycle.
    
    The only case that is not yet covered so far is when $w\in RR$, $z\in RB$ (or, respectively, when $z\in BR$) and $wz$ is a red edge.
    Invoking our assumption one last time, there must be a blue edge incident in $w$, say $ww'$.
    Crucially, we may assume that $w'\in RR$ since any other possibility is covered by the preceding discussions.
    Finally, the cycle $(w,w',y,z)$ (or, respectively $(w,w',x,z)$, when $z\in BR$) is balanced.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}\label{ex:balancing_numbers}~ 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The path on three edges, $P_4$, has balancing number $0$.
    
    Suppose that the edges of $K_n$ are coloured in red and blue with at least some edge of each colour.
    In particular, a red edge and a blue edge must intersect, forming a path of two edges of different colour.
    Taking any third edge that completes the copy of $P_4$, successfully builds a balanced copy regardless of the colour of the third edge.
    
    \item The graph consisting of four disjoint edges, $4K_2$, has balancing number $n-1$.
    Concretely, when $n\geq 10$, $\bal(n,4K_2)=n-1$.
    This is \cite[Theorem~2.3]{Caro_small_balanceable}.

    \item When $n\geq5$, $\bal(n,K_{1,4})=n-1$.
    This is an immediate consequence of \cite[Theorem~3.2]{unavoidable_chromatic}.

    \item The complete graph $K_5$ is not balanceable.
    
    Consider a colouring of $K_{2n}$ where we split the vertex set into two disjoint sets $A$ and $B$ of size $n$.
    We colour every edge connecting vertices in $A$ red, and edges connecting vertices in $B$ blue; edges connecting $A$ with $B$ are also red.
    Suppose a balanced copy $C$ of $K_5$ sits in $K_{2n}$ with vertex set $V(C)\subseteq V(K_{2n})$; then five of its ten edges must be blue.
    The vertex set $V(C)\cap B$ induces a complete graph (a clique in $K_{2n}$) such that an edge $xy\in E(C)$ is blue if and only if $\{x,y\}\subseteq B$.
    Since no complete graph has five edges, this is a contradiction.
    
    This example illustrates the characterization provided by Theorem~\ref{thm:bal_char}.
    The preceding argument shows that that $K_5$ fails the second condition of the theorem; it is straightforward (albeit unnecessary) to show that it also fails the first.
\end{enumerate}
\end{example}

Let me elucidate the case when the value of $\bal(n,G)$ is independent of the parameter $n$.
So, suppose that $G$ is a graph on $k$ edges (for simplicity, let's say $k$ is always even), such that $\bal(n,G)=C(G)$.
In such a case, I say that $G$ has \emph{constant balancing number}, such as in Example~\ref{ex:balancing_numbers}(1).
The graph $\frac k2K_2$ is a set of $k/2$ disjoint edges (a \emph{matching}), and $K_{1,k/2}$ is a \emph{star graph}, that is, $k/2$ edges incident on a single vertex.
Now, fix the complete graph $K_n$.
One can always pick an arbitrary vertex $v_0$ and colour all edges incident in $v_0$ red and every other edge of $K_n$ blue, as done in the proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:balC_4_is_one}.
Provided one makes $n$ large enough, one can guarantee that $K_n$ has more than $C(G)$ red edges.
By definition, there is a balanced copy of $G$ inside $K_n$.
In particular, all of its red edges (which account for exactly $k/2$ edges of $G$) intersect at some vertex.
This vertex in $G$ can be then chosen as the image of the center of the star graph $K_{1,k/2}$ under an injective graph homomorphism $K_{1,k/2}\to G$.

Consider a different colouring of $K_n$.
This time, pick a maximal matching in $K_n$ and colour those edges red, and everything else blue.
The size of the matching increases with $n$, and so once again, for large enough $n$, we can guarantee that $G$ satisfies Definition~\ref{def:balancing_num}.
Consequently, (at least) half of the edges of $G$ must be pairwise disjoint.
In other words, one can construct an injective homomorphism $\frac k2K_2\to G$.

As it turns out, these two aforementioned properties fully characterize graphs of constant balancing number.
In \cite{Caroetal2023}, we proved this result in parallel to the same characterization published in \cite[Section~3.2]{caro2022evolution}, where the value of $C(G)$ found involves Ramsey numbers, which grow exponentially.
In our paper, the characterization offers a deeper understanding of the structure of the graphs with constant balancing number, and furthermore shows that $\bal(n,G)$ is quadratic in $k$ (and not just bounded by a function exponential in $k$).
Moreover, we extend the characterization to graphs with an odd number of edges.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:constant_bal}
    Let $k>0$ be an even integer and $G$ a graph with $k$ edges and no isolated vertices.
    Then, the following are equivalent.
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item $G$ has constant balancing number.

        \item There are injective graph homomorphisms $\frac k2K_2\to G$ and $K_{1,k/2}\to G$.
        
        \item $$\bal(n,G)\leq\frac{(|V(G)|-3)(|V(G)|+k)}2.$$ 
    \end{enumerate}

    Moreover, if $G$ has either $k$ or $k+1$ edges, then $$\frac1{18}k^2-\frac{11}{18}k+\frac{14}9\leq\bal(n,G)\leq\frac{15}8k^2-\frac74k-1.$$
\end{theorem}

Implication (1)$\implies$(2) is \cite[Theorem~3.6]{caro2022evolution} proved using Ramsey numbers and also \cite[Theorem~1.1]{Caroetal2023} proved by an involved structural induction.
The implication (2)$\implies$(3) is \cite[Theorem~3.7]{Caroetal2023} and is the only part that requires that $G$ has no isolated vertices.
The moreover part is \cite[Theorem~5.1]{Caroetal2023}.
The implication (3)$\implies$(1) is immediate, as the bound in (3) does not depend on $n$.

If one wishes to study an analogue notion for infinite graphs, one has to define what it means for a large complete graph to have ``sufficiently many edges of each colour.''
In \cite[Section~4]{bowen2020unavoidable}, the authors study a somewhat similar notion of balanceability and suggest an approach to generalizing these ideas to the infinite case.
I draw inspiration from this approach and adapt some of the ideas to our theory.
Namely, that ``sufficiently many edges of each colour'' corresponds to non-meagre colour classes in a Polish space.
The basic notions regarding graphs defined on Polish spaces is covered in Section~\ref{sec:descr_combi_background}.

I prove an analog of Theorem~\ref{thm:constant_bal} that says that if the colours are well represented in a complete graph on a perfect Polish space, then one can find a star graph (in this context: a fixed centre connected to a Cantor set through edges) in one colour, joined with perfectly-many pairwise disjoint edges in the other colour; I believe this to be the right interpretation of the uncountable version of a graph with constant balancing number.

\begin{lemma}\label{lemma:graph_localization}
    Let $X$ be a perfect Polish space.
    Given non-meagre disjoint sets $A,B\subseteq[X]^2$ with the Baire property, there are disjoint open sets $U_A,U_B\subseteq X$ such that $A$ and $B$ are comeagre in $[U_A]^2$ and $[U_B]^2$, respectively.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
    Since $A$ and $B$ possess the Baire property, there are open sets of edges $O_A,O_B\subseteq[X]^2$ such that $A\Delta O_A$ and $B\Delta O_B$ are meagre.
    The fact that $A$ and $B$ are disjoint implies that $O_A\cap O_B\subseteq(A\Delta O_A)\cup(B\Delta O_B)$, and hence $O_A\cap O_B$ is meagre as well.
    Thus, by the Baire category theorem, $O_A$ and $O_B$ are disjoint open sets.

    Now let $\pi\colon X^2\to[X]^2$ be the quotient map and appreciate that $$\mathcal O_A:=\pi^{-1}[A]\quad\text{ and }\quad\mathcal O_B:=\pi^{-1}[B]$$ are disjoint, symmetric open subsets of the Polish space $X^2\setminus\{(x,x):x\in X\}$.
    Since $X$ is perfect, there are open pairwise disjoint sets $U_A^0,V_A^0,U_B^0,V_B^0\subseteq X$ such that $$V_A^0\times U_A^0,\; U_A^0\times V_A^0\subseteq\mathcal O_A\quad\text{ and }\quad V_B^0\times U_B^0,\; U_B^0\times V_B^0\subseteq\mathcal O_B.$$ So set $U_A:=U_A^0\cap V_A^0$ and $U_B:=U_B^0\cap V_B^0$, and notice that since $\mathcal O_A$ and $\mathcal O_B$ are open and disjoint, so are $U_A$ and $U_B$ open, disjoint and nonempty.

    Also, notice that $[U_A]^2\subseteq O_A$ and $[U_B]^2\subseteq O_B$.
    Finally, $[U_A]^2\setminus A\subseteq O_A\setminus A\subseteq A\Delta O_A$, and similarly $[U_B]^2\setminus B$, is meagre.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:inf_const_bal}
    Let $X$ be a perfect Polish space and suppose that $\chi\colon[X]^2\to2$ is a Baire measurable function with non-meager fibres (the colour classes).
    Then, there is a colour $i<2$, a vertex $c\in X$, and a Cantor set $C\subseteq X$ such that for all $x\in C$, $\chi(\{c,x\})=i$, and such that $[C]^2$ contains an uncountable matching of colour $1-i$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    Suppose that $\chi\colon[X]^2\to\{\text{red},\text{blue}\}$ is a Baire measurable function with non-meager fibres.
    Apply Lemma~\ref{lemma:graph_localization} to $A:=\chi^{-1}\{\text{red}\}$ and $B:=\chi^{-1}\{\text{blue}\}$ to obtain disjoint open sets $U_R$ and $U_B$ with $A$ comeagre in $[U_R]^2$ and $B$ comeagre in $[U_B]^2$.

    Since $\chi$ is Baire measurable, the sets of edges of each colour in $U_R\times U_B$ have the Baire property, so on some nonempty open set $U'_R\times U'_B\subseteq U_R\times U_B$, either the set of blue edges or the set of red edges is comeager.
    Without loss of generality, suppose it is blue.

    By the Kuratowski-Ulam theorem, the set $$B:=\{y\in U'_B:\{x\in U'_R:\chi(x,y)=\text{blue}\}\text{ is comeagre in }U'_R\}$$ is comeagre in $U'_B$, in particular nonempty, so choose $c\in B$ such that $S_c:=\{x\in U'_R:\chi(x,y)=\text{blue}\}$ is comeagre in $U'_R$.

    Since the set of red edges is comeagre in $U'_R$ and $\chi$ has the Baire property, Galvin's theorem gives a Cantor set $C_0\subseteq U'_R$ such that $[C_0]^2$ is red.
    Now find a Cantor set $C$ contained in $C_0\cap S_c$ and observe that $[C]^2$ is red and every edge $(c,x)$ for $x\in C$ is blue.

    Fix a homeomorphism $\varphi\colon 2^\omega\to C$.
    It follows that $\{(\varphi(0^\frown x),\varphi(1^\frown x):x\in 2^\omega)\}$ is a red matching.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The group of feasible edge replacements}\label{subsec:fer}

I start this subsection by setting up a combinatorial puzzle.
A graph is given, and one may remove an edge and add it wherever there is no edge (trivially, one can add it back to its original position), but the new graph must be isomorphic to the starting graph.
Such an action is called a \emph{feasible edge replacement}, which is formalized after these examples.
Can one attain every possible configuration of the graph by repeateadly changing edges like this?
It is clear that the answer depends on the structure of the particular starting graph.
For instance, a cycle of four edges has no valid non-trivial movements since, having removed an edge, the only possibility that preserves the cycle is putting the edge back from where it was taken.
Graphically, Figure~\ref{fig:4_cycles} depicts two isomorphic graphs that cannot be transformed into each other by feasible edge replacements.

\begin{definition}[Labeled graph]\label{def:labeled-graph}
    For a set $X$, whose elements are called \emph{labels}, denote by $S_X$ the set of all bijections, called \emph{label permutations}, $X\to X$.
    Under the operation of composition, $S_X$ forms a group called the \emph{symmetric group on $X$}.
    As usual, we use the notation $\rho\cdot\sigma:=\rho\circ\sigma$ for the group operation.
    Endow this set with the topology inherited from the Tychonoff product $X^X$, with $X$ taken as discrete.
    Let $G$ be a graph defined on a vertex set $V$ and let $\lambda\colon V\to X$ be a bijection, called a \emph{labeling} of $G$.
    For the sake of simplicity, I will always write vertices as $v_\ell\in V$ where $\lambda(v_\ell)=\ell\in X$ is the label.

    The pair $(G,\lambda)$ is called a \emph{labeled graph}, and the paremeters $V,E$ and $X$ are considered to be implicit.
\end{definition}

Every label permutation $\sigma\in S_X$ induces the graph isomorphism $\varphi_\sigma:=\lambda^{-1}\circ\sigma\circ\lambda\colon V\to V$ of $G$, and every graph isomorphism of $G$ of the form $\varphi\colon V\to V$ (that is, on the same vertex set) induces a label permutation $\sigma_\varphi:=\lambda\circ\varphi\circ\lambda^{-1}\in S_X$.
For this reason, label permutations and isomorphisms are often used interchangeably, but I do not follow this convention for the sake of clarity.

\begin{lemma}\label{lemma:varphi-iso-iff-image}
    Let $G$ and $G'$ be graphs on the same vertex set $V$.
    Given a bijection $\varphi\colon V\to V$, $\varphi$ is an isomorphism between $G$ and $G'$ if and only if $$\varphi[E(G)]=E(G').$$
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
    This is immediate from the definition of graph isomorphism: $\varphi$ is an isomorphism if and only if $v_iv_j\in E(G)\Leftrightarrow \varphi(v_i)\varphi(v_j)\in E(G')$, which is precisely the statement $\varphi[E(G)]=E(G')$.
\end{proof}

We adapt the notation from \cite{Caro2020GraphsIU} and all subsequent papers.
Given a labeled graph $(G,\lambda)$, the \emph{edge-label set of $G$} is defined as
\[
    L_G:=\{ij : v_iv_j\in E(G)\},
\]
where $ij$ and $ji$ are not distinguished.
Thus, $L_G$ is the set of labels induced on the edges of $G$ by $\lambda$.
For each $\sigma\in S_X$, define $G_\sigma$ to be the graph on the same vertex set $V$ with edge set
\[
    E(G_\sigma):=\{v_iv_j : \sigma(i)\sigma(j)\in L_G\}.
\]
In words, $G_\sigma$ is the unique graph on $V$ satisfying $L_{G_\sigma}=\sigma^{-1}[L_G]$, where $\sigma^{-1}[L_G]:=\{\sigma^{-1}(i)\sigma^{-1}(j):ij\in L_G\}$.
In particular, $L_{G_\sigma}=L_G$ for every $\sigma\in S_X$ (the set of edge labels is invariant across all copies), and $G_{\text{id}}=G$.

The relationship between $G_\sigma$ and $\varphi_\sigma$ is as follows.
By Lemma~\ref{lemma:varphi-iso-iff-image}, $\varphi_\sigma$ is an isomorphism $G\to G'$ if and only if $\varphi_\sigma[E(G)]=E(G')$.
Since $\varphi_\sigma(v_i)=v_{\sigma^{-1}(i)}$, this image equals $\{v_{\sigma^{-1}(i)}v_{\sigma^{-1}(j)}:v_iv_j\in E(G)\}$, whose edge-label set is exactly $\sigma^{-1}[L_G]=L_{G_\sigma}$.
In summary:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gsigma-varphi}
    \varphi_\sigma \text{ is an isomorphism } G\to G' \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad G_\sigma = G' \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \sigma^{-1}[L_G]=L_{G'}.
\end{equation}

Suppose that $G^c$ denotes the graph complement of $G$ (that is, the graph obtained from switching every adjacency and every non-adjacency in $G$) and let $(e,f)\in E(G)\times E(G^c)$, and $\sigma\in S_X$.
I use the phrase ``$\sigma::(e\to f)$ is a \emph{feasible edge replacement}'' to abbreviate the fact that $\varphi_\sigma$ is a graph isomorphism between $G$ and $G-e+f$, or equivalently, that $\sigma^{-1}[L_G]=L_{G-e+f}$.\footnote{I delay defining the object $\sigma::(e\to f)$ (Definition~\ref{def:fer-object}), for a few paragraphs, purely for expository purposes, as this requires an additional level of abstraction that would otherwise lack motivation.}
If $\varphi_\sigma\in\Aut(G)$, I employ the symbol $\sigma::(\emptyset\to\emptyset)$ to signal that no edge is being moved.
Denote by $\Fer(G)$ the group generated by all $\sigma$ for which there are $e$ and $f$ such that $\sigma::(e\to f)$ is a feasible edge replacement.
These label permutations $\sigma$ are called the \emph{canonical generators} of $\Fer(G)$, and they include permutations that induce automorphisms on $G$ (which can be visualized as an action that leaves the graph looking identical) and permutations that correspond to moving exactly one edge.
Thus, intuitively, each element of the group $\Fer(G)$ corresponds to a ``sequence'' of feasible edge replacements.

Let $$A(G):=\{\sigma\in S_X:\varphi_\sigma\in\Aut(G)\}\simeq\Aut(G)$$ be the subgroup of label permutations that correspond to the automorphisms of $G$.
Hence, the following subgroup structure holds.
$$1\leq A(G)\leq\Fer(G)\leq S_X,$$ where, as usual, the $1$ on the left represents the trivial subgroup of $S_X$ containing just the identity on $X$.

It is often convenient to represent a sequence of feasible edge replacements (that is, an element of $\Fer(G)$) as a product of canonical generators (which, as standard in Algebra, is written right-to-left) paired with a ``list of instructions'' explaining what edges to replace and in what order (which, as standard in English, is written left-to-right).
This requires defining some more symbols.
Before doing this, there is a technical caveat: the labels of the graph's vertices change after each canonical generator gets applied (the labels get permuted); but having the list of edge replacements in the graph's original labeling is desirable if, for example, one wishes to execute a solution to a puzzle by moving edges without having to keep track of what the corresponding permutations are (not to mention the extra effort needed to figure out what those permutations are in the first place).
To circumvent this, I give the following abstract definition (by ``abstract,'' I mean that the operation does not actually require knowledge of the graph $G$ on which it is defined).

\begin{definition}[Edge replacements as objects]\label{def:fer-object}
    Suppose that $\sigma\colon X\to X$ is label permutation and that $x,y,z,w\in X$, and possibly adding subscripts, are labels.
    An \emph{edge replacement} is a tuple of the form $(\sigma, (x, y, z, w))$ and we always use the notation $\sigma::(xy\to zw)$ to represent it.

    If $\{x,y\}=\{z,w\}$, I use the notation $\sigma::(\emptyset\to\emptyset)$ to emphasize that no edge has been moved.

    A \emph{sequence of edge replacements} is a tuple $(\sigma,(x_i,y_i,z_i,w_i)_{i\leq n})$ where $\sigma$ is again a label permutation, and $\{x_i,y_i,z_i,w_i:i\leq n\}\subseteq X$.
    I employ the symbol $$\sigma::(x_0y_0\to z_0w_0)(x_1y_1\to z_1w_1)\cdots(x_ny_n\to z_nw_n)$$ to refer to this object.
\end{definition}

Intuitively, $\sigma::(xy\to zw)$ refers to applying the permutation $\sigma$ to the labels of the vertices and moving the edge $v_xv_y$ to the position of the non-edge $v_zv_w$.\footnote{For readers previously familiar with this notion: the notation $\sigma::(xy\to zw)$ denotes that $\sigma\in\Fer_G(xy\to zw)$ in the sense of \cite{Caro2020GraphsIU}.}

\begin{definition}[Concatenating edge replacements]\label{def:concatenation-fer}
    Now suppose that \begin{align*}
    f_0=\sigma&::(x_0y_0\to w_0z_0)(x_1y_1\to z_1w_1)\cdots(x_ny_n\to z_nw_n)\qquad\text{ and}\\
    f_1=\rho&::(a_0b_0\to c_0d_0)(a_1b_1\to c_1d_1)\cdots(a_mb_m\to c_md_m)
    \end{align*}
    are both sequences of edge replacements.
    Then the \emph{product} $f_0*f_1$, is the sequence of edge replacements \begin{align*}
        f_0*f_1:=\rho\cdot\sigma::(x_0y_0\to w_0z_0)(x_1y_1\to z_1w_1)\cdots\\
        \cdots(x_ny_n\to z_nw_n)(\sigma^{-1}(a_0)\sigma^{-1}(b_0)\to \sigma^{-1}(c_0)\sigma^{-1}(d_0))\cdots\\
        \cdots(\sigma^{-1}(a_m)\sigma^{-1}(b_m)\to\sigma^{-1}(c_m)\sigma^{-1}(d_m)).
    \end{align*}
\end{definition}

In other words, the inverse permutation must be applied to the second sequence of labels to ensure that the final labeling is consistent with the original labeling of the graph.
This relabeling also has a very natural categorical interpretation, in the category of labeled graphs $(G,\lambda)$.
Each edge replacement corresponds to a morphism between labeled graphs, and a sequence of feasible edge replacements corresponds to compositions of such morphisms.

Now, I connect the two notions thus far defined in this subsection and prove that they are indeed consistent with one another.
The following proposition can be inductively generalized to arbitrary finite sequences of edge replacements.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:fer-is-well-defined}
    Let $(G,\lambda)$ be a labeled graph and suppose that $f_0=\sigma::(xy\to wz)$ and $f_1=\rho::(ab\to cd)$ are feasible edge replacements of $G$.
    Then, their product $$f_0*f_1=\rho\cdot\sigma::(xy\to zw)(\sigma^{-1}(a)\sigma^{-1}(b)\to\sigma^{-1}(c)\sigma^{-1}(d))$$ is such that the induced map $\varphi_{\rho\cdot\sigma}$ is an isomorphism between $G$ and $$(G-v_xv_y+v_zv_w)-v_{\sigma^{-1}(a)}v_{\sigma^{-1}(b)}+v_{\sigma^{-1}(c)}v_{\sigma^{-1}(d)}.$$
\end{proposition}

Products of feasible edge replacements are called \tfer \emph{objects}, and their label permutations are precisely the elements of $\Fer(G)$.
The preceding proposition shows that $f_0*f_1$ is a \tfer object, which we prove as follows.

\begin{proof}
    Denote $G':=G-v_xv_y+v_zv_w$ and $G'':=G-v_av_b+v_cv_d$.
    At the level of edge labels, this means
    $$
        L_{G'}=(L_G\setminus\{xy\})\cup\{zw\}\qquad\text{ and }\qquad L_{G''}=(L_G\setminus\{ab\})\cup\{cd\}.
    $$

    Since $f_0$ is a feasible edge replacement of $G$, by \eqref{eq:gsigma-varphi}, $G_\sigma=G'$, that is,
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:f0-label}
        \sigma^{-1}[L_G]=L_{G'}=(L_G\setminus\{xy\})\cup\{zw\}.
    \end{equation}
    Since $f_1$ is a feasible edge replacement of $G$, again by \eqref{eq:gsigma-varphi}, $G_\rho=G''$:
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:f1-label}
        \rho^{-1}[L_G]=L_{G''}=(L_G\setminus\{ab\})\cup\{cd\}.
    \end{equation}
    
    The goal is to compute $(\rho\cdot\sigma)^{-1}[L_G]$.
    Since $(\rho\cdot\sigma)^{-1}=\sigma^{-1}\circ\rho^{-1}$, applying the bijection $\sigma^{-1}$ to both sides of \eqref{eq:f1-label} gives
    \begin{align}
        (\rho\cdot\sigma)^{-1}[L_G]
        &= \sigma^{-1}\bigl[\rho^{-1}[L_G]\bigr] \nonumber\\
        &= \sigma^{-1}\bigl[(L_G\setminus\{ab\})\cup\{cd\}\bigr] \nonumber\\
        &= \bigl(\sigma^{-1}[L_G]\setminus\{\sigma^{-1}(a)\sigma^{-1}(b)\}\bigr)\cup\{\sigma^{-1}(c)\sigma^{-1}(d)\}.
        \label{eq:product-step}
    \end{align}
    Substituting \eqref{eq:f0-label} into \eqref{eq:product-step} yields
    $$
        (\rho\cdot\sigma)^{-1}[L_G]= \bigl(L_{G'}\setminus\{\sigma^{-1}(a)\sigma^{-1}(b)\}\bigr)\cup\{\sigma^{-1}(c)\sigma^{-1}(d)\} = L_{G'''},
    $$
    where $G''':=G'-v_{\sigma^{-1}(a)}v_{\sigma^{-1}(b)}+v_{\sigma^{-1}(c)}v_{\sigma^{-1}(d)}$.
    By \eqref{eq:gsigma-varphi}, this means $G_{\rho\cdot\sigma}=G'''$ or, in other words, that $\varphi_{\rho\cdot\sigma}$ is an isomorphism $G\to G'''$.
\end{proof}

With the multiplication I defined in Definition~\ref{def:concatenation-fer}, \tfer objects form their own group structure, which I call the \tFer group; clearly, it is isomorphic to $\Fer(G)$.
As far as I know, this group is not explicitly defined in the existing body of literature, and doing so has some practical advantages, especially so from an engineering perspective.

For instance, a solution to the puzzle is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:4_cycles_solution}, where each caption shows the canonical generator needed to obtain that step of the solution; and the \tfer object that solves the puzzle (with the same labels as in the figure) is the product of the three generators, resulting in
$$(2\ 3)::(1\ 2\to1\ 3)(0\ 1\to0\ 2)(2\ 3\to1\ 2).$$
It is worth noting that the permutation $(2\ 3)$ is not a canonical generator, as (at least) two edges must necessarily change their position.

For the remainder of the chapter, topological closures are always taken in the containing space $X^X$.
In symbols, if $A\subseteq X^X$, $\overline A:=\cl(A,X^X)$.

\begin{definition}[Local Amoeba]\label{def:local_amoeba}
    A graph $G$ is called a \emph{local amoeba} if $\overline{\Fer(G)}=S_X$.
\end{definition}

Hence, a local amoeba is precisely a graph that can be transformed into any copy of itself on the same vertex set by a (possibly infinite) sequence of feasible edge replacements.
This graph-theoretic notion and the name \emph{amoeba} first appeared in \cite{unavoidable_chromatic} as an interesting combinatorial object with strong balanceability properties, described in Subsection~\ref{subsec:amoebas_bal}.
When $X$ is finite, all subsets of $X^X$ are closed and thus the preceding definition simplifies to $\Fer(G)=S_X$, which is the algebraic notion introduced in \cite{Caro2020GraphsIU} as a tool to study these graphs and their properties.
The currently published body of research studies the case when $X$ is finite.

If $C_4$ and $P_4$ are the examples depicted in Figure~\ref{fig:4_cycles} and Figure~\ref{fig:4_paths}, respectively, then $\Fer(C_4)=\Aut(C_4)$ is the dihedral group $D_4$ of order $2\cdot4$, and $\Fer(P_4)=S_4$ is the symmetric group.
Therefore, $C_4$ is not a local amoeba, while $P_4$ is.

Complete graphs are all local amoebas for a rather uninteresting reason: their automorphism group is $S_X$ (any permutation preserves the adjecencies trivially, since any two distinct vertices are adjacent).

\begin{definition}
    A graph $G$ is called \emph{rigid} if $\overline{\Fer(G)}=\Aut(G)$.
    $G$ is called \emph{asymmetric} if $\Aut(G)$ is the trivial group consisting of the identity on $X$.
\end{definition}

One of the main concerns of this active field of research is finding and classifying interesting examples of graphs that are local amoebas.
For instance, constructing trees with arbitrary large maximum degree that are local amoebas is a curious problem, whose solution was the main precursor of \cite{Eslavaetal2025}, which I present in Subsection~\ref{subsec:new_recursive}.

In terms of Group Theory, $\Fer(G)$ carries all of the expressive power of finite groups.
Precisely, one can show that there is no theorem of the form ``for every finite graph $G$, the group $\Fer(G)$ has property $\varphi$,'' where $\varphi$ is a non-trivial property about groups.

After Frucht's 1939 breakthrough showing that every finite group is the automorphism group of some finite graph, he extended this result to the more restrictive class of cubic graphs (graphs in which every vertex has degree 3) in 1949 (see \cite{Frucht1949}).
This result, often referred to as Frucht's theorem, demonstrates that for any finite group $G$, there exists a cubic graph $X$ such that $\Aut(X)\simeq G$.

For a cubic graph $G$, and more generally for a $k$-regular graph with $k\geq2$, $G$ is rigid,
that is $\Fer(G)=\Aut(G)$.
This is a direct consequence of the fact that applying a non-trivial feasible edge replacement changes the degree of at least one vertex (it increases or decreases by exactly one, depending on whether an edge was added or removed to a particular vertex).
Put succintly:

\begin{proposition}
    For any finite group $\Gamma$, there is a graph $G$ such that $\Gamma$ is isomorphic to $\Fer(G)$.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Local amoeba trees with large maximum degree}\label{subsec:new_recursive}

Intuitively, if one is trying to transform one graph into another by means of feasible edge replacements, it may come in handy to have isolated vertices that allow new positions for the graph.
I previously mentioned that complete graphs are rigid graphs, meaning they have no non-trivial feasible edge replacements.
Adding isolated vertices to a complete graph destroys its rigidity, since now new movements are possible.

\begin{definition}\label{def:global_amoeba}
    A finite graph $G$ is called a \emph{global amoeba} if there is a $t\geq1$ such that $G\cup tK_1$ (that is, the graph obtained as the disjoint union of $G$ and $t$ many isolated vertices) is a local amoeba.
\end{definition}

Perhaps surprisingly, adding isolated vertices to an arbitrary graph does not necessarily increase the amount of feasible edge replacements; in fact, there is no general logical implication between a graph $G$ being a local amoeba and being a global amoeba.\footnote{With this in mind, one might try to suggest more descriptive names for these graphs that do not hint at this inexisting causal link, but the author chose to respect the terminology from the well-established literature.}
One case where such an implication does exist is for trees (see Corollary~\ref{cor:local_trees_are_global}).

The main result of \cite{Caroetal2023} is the following classification of global amoebas.
Notably, if a value of $t$ exists such that Definition~\ref{def:global_amoeba} holds, then $t=1$ is always the least such value, as the following theorem states.

\begin{theorem}[{\cite[Theorem~4.1]{Caroetal2023}}]\label{thm:classification_global}
    For any non-empy graph $G$, the following are equivalent.
    
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item $G$ is a global amoeba.
        
        \item $G\cup K_1$ is a local amoeba.
        
        \item Every orbit of $\Fer(G)$ has a label that corresponds to some vertex of degree 1.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

A well-known fact about any finite tree $T$ with at least an edge is that it must have at least two vertices of degree 1.
Recalling that if $T$ is a local amoeba, then $\Fer(T)$ consists of a single orbit (it is in fact, the whole symmetric group), it is clear that condition (3) of Theorem~\ref{thm:classification_global} is satisfied for any tree $T$ that happens to be a local amoeba.

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:local_trees_are_global}
    If $T$ is a finite tree for which $\Fer(T)$ acts transitively on $T$ (for example if $T$ is a local amoeba), then $T$ is a global amoeba.
\end{corollary}

The connectedness of $T$ is indispensable.
For example, a matching (a set of pairwise disjoint edges) forms a global amoeba, since every vertex has degree 1, but not a local amoeba (it is, in fact, rigid).
The acyclic hypothesis on $T$ is also crucial: any cycle $C$ is rigid but $\Fer(C)$ acts transitively on it.

Figure~\ref{fig:global_non_local} depicts the smallest tree $T$ that is a global amoeba but not a local amoeba.
Interestingly, its \tFer group is transitive (that is, it consists of a single orbit).
There are also global amoeba trees whose \tFer group is not transitive, and the smallest such example has two orbits, depicted in Figure~\ref{fig:multi_orbit_tree} with vertices coloured by orbit under the action of the \tFer group.\footnote{Recall from elementary Group Theory that the set of orbits of a group action forms a partition of the set.
Thus, in this example, two vertices have the same colour if and only if there is a \tfer object that sends one to the other.}
The main conjecture of Section~\ref{sec:amoeba_trees} is that the \tFer groups of all global amoeba trees have at most two orbits.

The construction I describe now (see Figure~\ref{fig:Tk}), loosely inspired by the Fibonacci sequence, illustrates the main results of \cite{Eslavaetal2025}.
Start by defining $T_1$ and $T_2$ to each be the tree consisting of a single edge, in other words, $T_1\simeq T_2\simeq K_2$.
Now, suppose that $T_{k-1}$ and $T_{k-2}$ have already been constructed.
In each of these trees, select a vertex of maximum degree.
The tree $T_k$ is the result of joining $T_{k-1}$ and $T_{k-2}$ by the edge connecting these two vertices.
The choice of the vertex of maximum degree is irrelevant, as one can easily verify: either such vertex is unique, or there are exactly two such vertices that are similar (that is, they can be interchanged via an automorphism), and thus the resulting sequence of graphs is unique up to isomorphisms.

The sequence of graphs $\{T_k:k\in\mathbb{N}\}$ is a particular case of a more general scheme of recursive constructions studied in \cite[Section~3]{Eslavaetal2025}.

\begin{theorem}[{\cite[Theorem~9]{Eslavaetal2025}}]\label{thm:fibonacci_is_local}
    For every $k\in\mathbb{N}$, $T_k$ is a local amoeba.
\end{theorem}

An independent contribution of mine answers the following question which I developed in \cite[Section~5]{Eslavaetal2025}.
The proof that a finite graph $G$ is a local amoeba often involves a counting argument: one shows that $\Fer(G)$ has $|X|!$ elements and then, given that $\Fer(G)$ is contained in the symmetric group on $X$, $\Fer(G)$ must be equal to $S_X$.
There are two disadvantages with this method.

\begin{itemize}
    \item Given an arbitrary permutation $\sigma\in S_X$ (that is, an arbitrary copy of $G$ on the same vertex set), the proof generally offers no method of finding the sequence of feasible edge replacements.
    In other words, this proving method shows the existence of a solution to the puzzle, without giving an algorithm to find it.
    The algorithm presented in Subsection~\ref{subsec:algorithm_Tk} resolves this for an infinite family of trees.
    
    \item Counting methods don't generalize to infinite graphs.
    I address this in Section~\ref{sec:infinitary_amoeba_graphs}.
\end{itemize}

A few more notions come in handy not just for the upcoming section, but also for Section~\ref{sec:infinitary_amoeba_graphs}.

\begin{definition}\label{def:stem-symmetric}
    Let $(G,\lambda)$ be any labeled graph, $i\in X$ a label, and $\Gamma:=\Fer(G)$.
    The subgroup of $\Gamma$ generated by the canonical generators of $\Gamma$ that fix $i$ is denoted by $\Fer^i(G)$.
    That is, in symbols, $\Fer^i(G):=\Gamma\cap\Stab_\Gamma(i)$.

    We say a graph $G$ is \emph{stem-symmetric with respect to the label $i$} (equivalently, \emph{stem-symmetric with respect to the vertex $\lambda(i)$}) if $\Fer^i(G)=\{\varphi\in S_X:\varphi(i)=i\}$.
\end{definition}

In other words, a finite graph $G=(V,E)$ is stem-symmetric with respect to a vertex $v$ if, for any isomorphism $\varphi\colon V\to V$ of $G$ that fixes $v$, one can attain the graph $G_\varphi$ by a sequence of \tfer objects that all fix the label of $v$.

To convince the reader that this defines a non-trivial condition on $G$, consider the example of a path $P_n$ on an odd number of vertices $n\geq3$.
Let $k=\frac{n-1}2$ be the midpoint.
If $\rho$ is the automorphism that reflects $P_n$, that is $\rho\colon i\mapsto n-i-1$, then $\rho$ fixes the midpoint $k$ and $\Aut(P_n)=\{\id_n,\rho\}\simeq S_2$.
Given a canonical generator $\sigma::(e\to f)\in\Fer^k(P_n)$, the only choice of edges that lets $\sigma(k)=k$ is when $e=f$, and hence $\sigma\in A(P_n)$.
This proves that $\Fer^k(P_n)=A(P_n)\simeq S_2$, but as per a previous discussion, $\Fer(P_n)=S_n$ (all paths are local amoebas).
For a concrete example, the transposition that interchanges the endpoints, $(0\ (n-1))$ fixes the midpoint, but is not attainable as a sequence of \tfer objects that all fix the midpoint.
Therefore, $P_n$ is not stem-symmetric with respect to $k$.
The situation is different when picking a different vertex $k$ in a path.

\begin{lemma}\label{lemma:path-stem-symmetric}
    For every $n\in\mathbb N$, the path $P_{n+1}$ is stem-symmetric with respect to the endpoint $n$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
    Let $1\leq\ell<n$ and notice that the graph $P_{n+1}-(\ell,\ell+1)+(0,\ell+1)$ is a path on $n+1$ vertices and thus isomorphic to $P_{n+1}$.
    It is possible to find an explicit isomorphism that fixes $n$ and decompose it into transpositions.
    In symbols, $$\prod_{k=0}^\ell(k\quad\ell-k)::((\ell,\ell+1)\to(0,\ell+1))$$ is a \tfer object that realizes this isomorphism.
    
    The second step is observing that, letting $\ell$ vary, the preceding permutations generate the symmetric group on (the set) $n$.
    This is straightforward and can be done in multiple ways.
    For example, every transposition of the form $(0\ \ell)$, for $1\leq\ell<n$, is in $\Fer^n(P_{n+1})$ by an inductive argument on $\ell$, taking conjugates, and noticing that the transposition in question is the left-most factor in the $\ell$-th permutation above.
\end{proof}

\subsection{A recursive algorithm for Fibonacci-type trees}\label{subsec:algorithm_Tk}

I now describe an algorithm that, given any permutation $\sigma\in S_X$ on the vertices of $T_k$, produces an explicit sequence of feasible edge replacements whose composition realises $\sigma$.
Throughout this section, fix a labeling of $T_k$ and write $n = n(k) = 2F_k$ for its order, where $F_k$ is the $k$-th Fibonacci number.

\paragraph{Recursive decomposition of $T_k$.}
The proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:fibonacci_is_local} proceeds by induction and uses the following structural decomposition, which I now make explicit so that the algorithm can be stated precisely.

For $k \ge 5$, the tree $T_k$ is built from four subtrees $H_1, J_1, H_2, J_2$ with
$$
    H_1 \simeq T_{k-3},\quad
    J_1 \simeq T_{k-2},\quad
    H_2 \simeq T_{k-3},\quad
    J_2 \simeq T_{k-4},
$$
whose vertex sets are pairwise disjoint, by adding three edges:
$$
    T_k
    \;=\;
    \bigl((H_1 \cup J_1) + v_a v_b\bigr)
    \cup
    \bigl((H_2 \cup J_2) + v_c v_d\bigr)
    + v_b v_c,
$$
where $v_a, v_b, v_c, v_d$ are the vertices of maximum degree in $H_1, J_1, H_2, J_2$, respectively.
Call $a = \lambda(v_a),\, b = \lambda(v_b),\, c = \lambda(v_c),\,d = \lambda(v_d)$ the \emph{roots} of $T_k$ and denote by
$$
    A = \lambda[V(H_1)],\quad
    B = \lambda[V(J_1)],\quad
    C = \lambda[V(H_2)],\quad
    D = \lambda[V(J_2)]
$$
the corresponding label sets.
These four sets partition $X$, and $b$ is the label of the unique vertex of maximum degree in $T_k$.
Now fix once and for all a label $y_0 \in B \setminus \{b\}$.

Two feasible edge replacements in $T_k$ play a distinguished role.
\begin{itemize}
    \item The replacement $cd\to ad$ induces a bijection $\rho\colon A\to C$ that extends to the isomorphism $H_1\to H_2$ sending $v_a\mapsto v_c$, while fixing $B \cup D$ pointwise.
    
    \item The replacement $ab\to ac$ induces a bijection $\varphi\colon B\to C\cup D$ that extends to the isomorphism $(H_2\cup J_2)+v_cv_d\to J_1$ sending $v_b\mapsto v_c$, while fixing $A$ pointwise.
\end{itemize}
Both $\rho$ and $\varphi$ are therefore canonical generators of $\Fer(T_k)$.

\paragraph{The algorithm.}
It suffices to produce a \tfer object for every transposition of the form $(y_0, x)$ with $x \in X \setminus \{y_0\}$, since any permutation $\sigma \in S_X$ can be written as a product of such transpositions via its cycle decomposition:
$$
    (a_0, a_1, \ldots, a_m)
    \;=\;
    (y_0, a_0)(y_0, a_1) \cdots (y_0, a_m)(y_0, a_0).
$$
The function $\RecFer(x, k)$, given in Algorithm~\ref{alg:RecursiveFer}, returns a \tfer object for $(y_0, x)$ by induction on $k$, using the decomposition above.
The four cases correspond to which of the label sets $A, B, C, D$ the input $x$ belongs to.
The key identities driving each case are:
\begin{align*}
    x \in A \cup B &\implies (y_0, x) \in \Fer^b(T_{k-1}),
    \quad\text{by the stem-symmetry of } T_{k-1},\\
    x = b &\implies (y_0, x) = \varphi^{-1}(y_0,\, \varphi^{-1}(b))\,\varphi, \\
    x \in C &\implies (y_0, x) = \rho^{-1}(y_0,\, \rho^{-1}(x))\,\rho,
    \quad \rho^{-1}(x) \in A, \\
    x \in D&\implies (y_0, x) = (y_0, x_0)(x, x_0)(y_0, x_0),
    \quad x_0 \in C \text{ a fixed neighbor of } c.
\end{align*}

\begin{algorithm}[ht]
    \DontPrintSemicolon
    \caption{Factoring a permutation into a \tFer sequence for $T_k$.}
    \label{alg:RecursiveFer}
    \SetKwFunction{FRF}{RecursiveFer}
    \SetKwProg{Fn}{Function}{:}{}
    @cache\tcp*{Memoize over all $(x,k)$ pairs across the entire execution.}
    \Fn{\FRF{$x$, $k$}}{
        \lIf{$k \le 4$}{\KwRet $\texttt{hashMap}[(y_0,x)]$}
        Let $a,b,c,d$ and $A,B,C,D$ be the roots and label sets of $T_k$\;
        \uIf{$x \in A \cup B$}{
            \KwRet \FRF{$x$,\,$k-1$}\;
        }\uElseIf{$x = b$}{
            \KwRet $\varphi *$ \FRF{$\varphi^{-1}(b)$,\,$k$} $* \varphi^{-1}$\;
        }\uElseIf{$x \in C$}{
            \KwRet $\rho *$ \FRF{$\rho^{-1}(x)$,\,$k-1$} $* \rho^{-1}$\;
        }\Else{
            \tcp{$x \in D$}
            Let $x_0 \in C$ be a fixed neighbor of $c$\;
            $F_0 \leftarrow$ \FRF{$x_0$,\,$k$}\;
            $F_1 \leftarrow$ \FRF{$x-c$,\,$k-2$} $+\, c$\tcp*{Shift all labels by $c$.}
            \KwRet $F_0 * F_1 * F_0$\;
        }
    }
    \smallskip
    \KwIn{$k$ and $\sigma\in S_X$.}
    \tcc{Step 1. Decompose $\sigma$ into transpositions $(y_0,x)$.}
    $\mathrm{trans} \leftarrow [\,]$\;
    \For{$(a_0,\ldots,a_m)$ cycle of $\sigma$}{
        Append $(y_0,a_i)$ for $i=0,\ldots,m$, then append $(y_0,a_0)$\;
    }
    \tcc{Step 2. Compute a \tFer object per transposition (memoized).}
    \For{$(y_0,x) \in \mathrm{trans}$}{
        $\texttt{hashMap}[(y_0,x)] \leftarrow$ \FRF{$x$,\,$k$}\;
    }
    \tcc{Step 3. Form the product.}
    \KwOut{$$F = \prod_{(y_0,x)\,\in\,\mathrm{trans}} \texttt{hashMap}[(y_0,x)]$$}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{The shallow transposition improvement.}
The $D$-case triple product $$(y_0,x)=(y_0,x_0)(x,x_0)(y_0,x_0),$$ if applied naively, doubles the contribution of $(y_0,x_0)$ at every recursion level and leads to a sequence length satisfying
$\ell(k) = \Theta(k^2)$.
This can be improved by observing that every transposition $(y_0,x)$ can instead be built by \emph{tracing the unique path} from $x$ to $y_0$ in $T_k$.
Each step of this path crosses exactly one subtree boundary, and each boundary crossing can be implemented using a constant number of feasible edge replacements: either a single application of $\rho$ or $\varphi$, which are each induced by a single edge replacement.
Since the depth of $T_k$ is $O(k)$ and the path from $x$ to $y_0$ crosses at most $O(k)$ subtree boundaries, the resulting sequence for $(y_0,x)$ has length $O(k)$.
We refer to this as the \emph{shallow transposition routine}.

\paragraph{Correctness and complexity.}

\begin{theorem}[Correctness]\label{thm:algorithm_correct}
    For every $k \ge 1$ and every permutation $\sigma \in S_X$ on the vertices of $T_k$, Algorithm~\ref{alg:RecursiveFer} terminates and outputs a sequence of feasible edge replacements of $T_k$ whose permutation is $\sigma$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    I prove by strong induction on $k$ that $\RecFer(x,k)$ returns a \tfer object whose permutation is $(y_0,x)$ and whose edge replacement sequence is feasible in $T_k$.

    For $k\leq 4$ this holds by assumption on the precomputed base cases.
    Let $k\geq 5$ and suppose the claim holds for all smaller values.

    \emph{Case $x \in A \cup B$.}
    The labels $A \cup B = \lambda[V(T_{k-1})]$ form the vertex set of the subtree $H = (H_1 \cup J_1) + v_a v_b \cong T_{k-1}$, and $y_0 \in B\subseteq A \cup B$.
    By the induction hypothesis, $\RecFer(x, k-1)$ produces a feasible sequence in $T_{k-1}$ realising $(y_0,x)$.
    Any feasible edge replacement of a subgraph $H \subseteq T_k$ that fixes the label $b$ of the connecting vertex $v_b$ is also a feasible edge replacement of $T_k$ (this is the content of \cite[Lemma~5]{Eslavaetal2025}).
    Since every element of $\Fer^b(T_{k-1})$ fixes $b$, the sequence remains feasible in $T_k$.

    \emph{Case $x = b$.}
    We have $\varphi^{-1}(b) \in A \cup B$ (since $\varphi \colon B \to C \cup D$ maps $v_b \mapsto v_c$ and hence $b \mapsto c \notin B$, so $\varphi^{-1}(b) \in A$).
    The recursive call $\RecFer(\varphi^{-1}(b), k)$ falls into the $A \cup B$ case and is correct by the above.
    The conjugation identity $(y_0, b) = \varphi^{-1} \cdot (y_0, \varphi^{-1}(b)) \cdot \varphi$
    holds in $S_X$, and $\varphi$ corresponds to a single feasible edge replacement, so the concatenated sequence is feasible.

    \emph{Case $x \in C$.}
    Since $\rho \colon A \to C$ is a bijection and $\rho^{-1}(x) \in A \subseteq A \cup B$, the recursive call falls into the $A \cup B$ case.
    The conjugation identity $(y_0, x) = \rho^{-1} \cdot (y_0, \rho^{-1}(x)) \cdot \rho$ holds in $S_X$, and $\rho$ is a single feasible edge replacement.

    \emph{Case $x \in D$.}
    Since $x_0 \in C$, the object $F_0 = \RecFer(x_0, k)$ is correct by the $C$-case above.
    The shift $\RecFer(x-c, k-2) + c$ embeds the subtree $J_2 \cong T_{k-2}$ (with labels shifted by $c$) into $D$; by the induction hypothesis this produces a feasible sequence for $(x_0, x)$ within $D$, which remains
    feasible in $T_k$ by the same extension lemma.
    The identity $(y_0,x)=(y_0,x_0)(x,x_0)(y_0,x_0)$ holds in $S_X$ since $y_0, x_0, x$ are distinct, and the corresponding concatenation of three feasible sequences is feasible.
    
    Having verified all cases of $\RecFer$, the correctness of the main program follows: steps~1--3 apply the standard factorisation of $\sigma$ into transpositions $(y_0, x_i)$ and compose the corresponding \tFer
    objects, yielding a feasible sequence for $\sigma$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[{\cite[Theorem~9]{Eslavaetal2025}}]\label{cor:Tk_local_amoeba}
    For every $k\geq 1$, $T_k$ is a local amoeba.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
    By Theorem~\ref{thm:algorithm_correct}, for every $\sigma\in S_X$ there exists a sequence of feasible edge replacements of $T_k$ whose permutation is $\sigma$, and hence $\sigma\in\Fer(T_k)$.
    Therefore $\Fer(T_k)=S_X$, which is the definition of a local amoeba.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Complexity]\label{thm:algorithm_complexity}
    Using the shallow transposition routine, Algorithm~\ref{alg:RecursiveFer} runs in time and space $O(n \log n)$, where $n = 2F_k = |V(T_k)|$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    Since $n = 2F_k$ and $F_k = \Theta(\phi^k)$ for the golden ratio $\phi$, we have $k = \Theta(\log n)$.

    \medskip
    \noindent\textit{Template count per transposition.}
    With the shallow transposition routine, $\RecFer(x,k)$ traces the path from $x$ to $y_0$ in $T_k$, contributing $O(1)$ templates per recursion level.
    The path crosses at most $k$ subtree boundaries, giving $\len(\RecFer(x,k)) = O(k)$ for every $x$.

    \medskip
    \noindent\textit{Number of distinct memoized calls.}
    With a global cache shared across all transpositions, every call is identified by a pair $(x, q)$ with $q \le k$.
    For fixed $q$ the number of possible values of $x$ is $n(q) = 2F_q$.
    Since
    $$
        \sum_{q=1}^{k} n(q)
        \;=\;
        2\sum_{q=1}^{k} F_q
        \;=\; 2(F_{k+2}-1)
        \;=\; O(F_k) = O(n),
    $$
    the cache contains $O(n)$ distinct entries.

    \medskip
    \noindent\textit{Total cost.}
    Each of the $O(n)$ cached calls performs $O(k)$ work, giving total template cost $O(nk) = O(n \log n)$.
    Steps~1 and~3 (cycle decomposition and the final product) each take $O(n)$.
    Hence the total running time and space are both $O(n \log n)$.

    \medskip
    \noindent\textit{Comparison with the published bound.}
    The analysis in \cite[Theorem~11]{Eslavaetal2025} establishes a $\Theta(n^2)$ bound by tracking the cost of $\RecFer(x,k)$ for a single transposition with a cold cache and then multiplying by the $O(n)$
    transpositions in the worst-case permutation.
    This is correct as stated, but it does not account for the savings from the global memoization shared across all transpositions in a single run of the algorithm, nor does it use the shallow transposition
    routine.
    The bound $O(n \log n)$ is therefore a genuine improvement in the algorithm.
\end{proof}
 
\begin{remark}
    The space bound $O(n \log n)$ is consistent with the space complexity stated in \cite[Theorem~11]{Eslavaetal2025}, which already observes $O(n \log n)$ space; the contribution here is that the same quantity also bounds the running time.
\end{remark}
